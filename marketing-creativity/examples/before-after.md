## Example 1: The Bakery

**Client context:**
- Started by a grandmother who was tired of "fake bread"
- Voice: 75 casual, 60 serious, 85 respectful, 70 enthusiastic
- Key quote: "I just want people to taste what bread SHOULD taste like"

### Website Headline

**Generic (Before):**
> "Welcome to [Bakery]. We offer fresh, high-quality baked goods made with the finest ingredients."

**With Voice (After):**
> "Your grandmother was right. Bread shouldn't taste like this. We're bringing it back."

**Why it works:** Uses the founder's actual motivation, creates intrigue, has personality.

## Example 2: The Accountant

**Client context:**
- Started after watching small businesses get crushed by bad financial advice
- Voice: 30 casual, 70 formal, 80 respectful, 50 matter-of-fact
- Key quote: "I've seen too many good businesses die because nobody explained things clearly"

### Email Subject Line

**Generic (Before):**
> "Tax Planning Strategies for Small Businesses"

**With Voice (After):**

## The Transformation Checklist

When rewriting generic copy, ask:

1. **Where's the founder's voice?** Can I use their actual words?
2. **Where's the story?** Is there a specific moment I can reference?
3. **Where's the emotion?** What does the customer FEEL?
4. **Where's the conflict?** What are we fighting against?
5. **Where's the surprise?** Is there anything unexpected?
6. **Would anyone write this?** Or does it sound like THEM?

## Red Flags: Signs It's Still Generic

- "High-quality" (says nothing)
- "Streamlined" (corporate speak)
- "Modern" (meaningless)
- "Passionate about..." (everyone says this)
- "We believe in..." without showing what that means
- No specific stories or quotes
- Could be any competitor's copy
- No personality or voice
- No conflict or tension
- No emotional hook in the first line
